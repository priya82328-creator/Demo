Sickle Cell Anemia

A genetic blood disorder caused by mutation in the HBB gene that changes hemoglobin structure and affects oxygen transport in the body.

What is Sickle Cell Anemia?

Sickle Cell Anemia is an inherited blood disorder where red blood cells become hard, sticky, and shaped like a sickle (crescent moon).

These abnormal cells block blood flow in small vessels, reduce oxygen delivery, and break down faster than normal cells.

Gene Involved

The disease is caused by a mutation in the HBB gene located on chromosome 11. This gene produces beta-globin, a key part of hemoglobin.

A single point mutation changes normal hemoglobin (HbA) into abnormal hemoglobin (HbS).

When oxygen levels are low, HbS causes red blood cells to become rigid and sickle-shaped, leading to blockages and tissue damage.

Symptoms

Pain Crises

Severe pain due to blocked blood flow.

Chronic Anemia

Fatigue due to rapid RBC breakdown.

Swelling

Painful swelling in hands and feet.

Infections

Weak immunity increases infection risk.

Delayed Growth

Low oxygen slows development.

Vision Issues

Blocked vessels may damage eyes.

Inheritance Pattern

Sickle Cell Anemia follows an autosomal recessive inheritance pattern.

A person must inherit two mutated HBB genes (one from each parent) to develop the disease.

A person with only one copy is a carrier (sickle cell trait) and usually does not show symptoms.

If both parents are carriers:

  • 25% chance of child having disease
  • 50% chance of being carrier
  • 25% chance of being normal

Treatment

There is no complete cure yet, but treatments help manage symptoms and improve quality of life.

  • Pain relief: Medications for crisis pain management.
  • Hydroxyurea: Reduces sickle cell formation.
  • Blood transfusions: Replace abnormal RBCs with healthy ones.
  • Hydration & oxygen: Improves circulation and reduces complications.
  • Bone marrow transplant: Potential curative option in severe cases.
  • Vaccination & antibiotics: Prevent infections.
