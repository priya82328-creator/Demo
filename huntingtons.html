Huntington's Disease

A progressive genetic neurodegenerative disorder caused by mutation in the HTT gene, leading to movement disorders, cognitive decline, and behavioral changes.

What is Huntington's Disease?

Huntington’s Disease is a hereditary brain disorder that causes progressive degeneration of nerve cells in the brain.

It affects movement, thinking ability, and emotions, gradually worsening over time.

Gene Involved (HTT Gene)

Huntington’s Disease is caused by a mutation in the HTT gene located on chromosome 4 (4p16.3).

The HTT gene normally produces the huntingtin protein, which is essential for neuron function and brain development.

In affected individuals, there is an abnormal expansion of CAG repeats. Normal range is 10–35 repeats, but in Huntington’s disease it increases to 36–120+ repeats.

This results in a toxic form of huntingtin protein that damages neurons, especially in the basal ganglia, leading to motor and cognitive symptoms.

A key feature is genetic anticipation, where the disease becomes more severe and appears earlier in successive generations.

Symptoms

Chorea

Uncontrolled jerking movements.

Memory Loss

Progressive cognitive decline.

Mood Changes

Depression, irritability, anxiety.

Speech Difficulty

Slurred speech and swallowing issues.

Loss of Coordination

Balance and walking problems.

Inheritance Pattern

Huntington’s Disease follows an autosomal dominant inheritance pattern.

Only one mutated copy of the HTT gene is enough to cause the disease.

If one parent is affected, each child has a 50% chance of inheriting the mutation.

The disease often appears between ages 30–50, but may occur earlier in cases of high CAG expansion.

Genetic anticipation:

  • Expansion of CAG repeats across generations
  • Earlier onset in children than parents
  • Increasing severity in next generation

Diagnosis

  • Genetic testing for HTT mutation
  • Neurological examination
  • MRI brain imaging
  • Cognitive testing

Treatment

  • No permanent cure available
  • Medications for movement control
  • Antidepressants for mood symptoms
  • Speech and physical therapy
  • Psychological support
