Thalassemia

Thalassemia is a genetic blood disorder caused by reduced production of hemoglobin, leading to anemia and abnormal red blood cells.

What is Thalassemia?

Thalassemia is an inherited disorder where the body produces less hemoglobin than normal. This causes red blood cells to break down easily and leads to chronic anemia.

Genes Involved

Thalassemia is caused by mutations in:

  • HBB gene → Beta Thalassemia
  • HBA1 / HBA2 genes → Alpha Thalassemia

These genes control hemoglobin chain production. Mutations reduce hemoglobin levels.

Inheritance Pattern

Thalassemia follows an autosomal recessive inheritance pattern.

A person must inherit two faulty genes (one from each parent) to develop the disease.

Carriers usually have mild or no symptoms.

Symptoms

Fatigue

Due to low hemoglobin levels.

Pale Skin

Reduced oxygen delivery in blood.

Slow Growth

Common in children with severe cases.

Bone Deformities

Bone marrow expansion due to anemia.

Diagnosis

  • Complete Blood Count (CBC)
  • Hemoglobin electrophoresis
  • Genetic testing (HBB/HBA mutations)
  • Peripheral blood smear

Treatment

  • Regular blood transfusions
  • Iron chelation therapy
  • Bone marrow transplant (curative)
  • Folic acid supplements
