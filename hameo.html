Hemophilia

A genetic bleeding disorder caused by deficiency of clotting factors, leading to excessive bleeding even from minor injuries.

What is Hemophilia?

Hemophilia is a rare inherited disorder where blood does not clot properly due to missing or defective clotting factors.

This leads to prolonged bleeding after injuries, surgery, or even spontaneous internal bleeding.

Gene Involved

Hemophilia is mainly caused by mutations in:

  • F8 gene → Hemophilia A (Factor VIII deficiency)
  • F9 gene → Hemophilia B (Factor IX deficiency)

These genes are responsible for producing clotting proteins essential for forming blood clots.

When these genes are mutated, clotting factors are reduced or absent, leading to uncontrolled bleeding.

Symptoms

Excess Bleeding

Prolonged bleeding from cuts or injuries.

Joint Pain

Bleeding into joints causing swelling.

Bruising Easily

Large bruises from minor injuries.

Nose Bleeds

Frequent and hard-to-stop nosebleeds.

Internal Bleeding

Dangerous bleeding inside body organs.

Inheritance Pattern

Hemophilia follows an X-linked recessive inheritance pattern.

The gene is located on the X chromosome, so males are mostly affected because they have only one X chromosome.

Females are usually carriers because they have two X chromosomes.

Key inheritance rules:

  • Male (XY) with mutated X → affected
  • Female (XX) with one mutation → carrier
  • Father cannot pass disease to son
  • Carrier mother → 50% chance of affected son

Diagnosis

  • Blood clotting tests (aPTT)
  • Factor VIII and IX level testing
  • Genetic testing for F8/F9 mutation
  • Family history analysis

Treatment

  • Clotting factor replacement therapy
  • Preventive factor infusions
  • Desmopressin (for mild Hemophilia A)
  • Gene therapy (emerging treatment)
  • Avoid injury-prone activities

Distribution of mild, moderate, and severe cases.
